Why this dataset?

The 2025 January 28 TidyTuesday dataset covers water insecurity across U.S. counties in 2023 specifically the share of households that lack complete indoor plumbing. At first glance this might look like a geography or public health dataset, but it’s actually a near-perfect case study for program evaluation. Access to clean water is shaped by race, poverty, and political geography in ways that are deeply confounded. You can’t just run a regression of “plumbing access” on income and call it a causal estimate there are too many backdoors. This makes the dataset ideal for applying the tools from this course: DAGs to map the confounding structure, regression adjustment to close backdoors, and a discussion of what it would take to actually identify a causal effect.

# Load TidyTuesday dataset
tuesdata <- tidytuesdayR::tt_load("2025-01-28")

water_insecurity_2023 <- tuesdata$water_insecurity_2023
water <- tuesdata[[1]]
water_clean <- water |>
  drop_na() |>
  # Derive some useful columns for analysis
  mutate(
    # Log of total population — large counties span orders of magnitude
    log_pop = log(total_pop),
    # Bin counties into four population quartiles for grouped comparisons
    pop_quartile = ntile(total_pop, 4),
    pop_quartile_label = factor(
      pop_quartile,
      labels = c("Smallest 25%", "25–50%", "50–75%", "Largest 25%")
    ),
    # Rough Census region from state FIPS — useful for faceting
    state_fips = as.integer(str_sub(geoid, 1, 2)),
    region = case_when(
      state_fips %in% c(9,23,25,33,44,50,34,36,42) ~ "Northeast",
      state_fips %in% c(17,18,26,39,55,19,20,27,29,31,38,46) ~ "Midwest",
      state_fips %in% c(10,11,12,13,24,37,45,51,54,1,21,28,47,5,22,40,48) ~ "South",
      state_fips %in% c(4,8,16,30,32,35,49,56,2,6,15,41,53) ~ "West",
      TRUE ~ "Other"
    ) |> factor(levels = c("Northeast", "Midwest", "South", "West"))
  ) |>
  filter(region != "Other")   # drops territories

A DAG for the causal structure

This is where the program evaluation framing kicks in. Suppose we want to ask: does population size a proxy for urban infrastructure investment causally reduce water insecurity? Or more precisely, what would happen to a county’s plumbing access rate if its population changed, holding everything else constant?

We can’t answer that with a raw correlation, because larger counties also tend to be wealthier, located in different regions, and have more political capacity to demand infrastructure investment. All of those factors independently affect plumbing access. We need to map the backdoors before we can close them.

water_dag <- dagify(
  plumbing    ~ population + region + poverty,
  population  ~ region,
  poverty     ~ region,
  plumbing    ~ poverty,
  outcome  = "plumbing",
  exposure = "population",
  labels = list(
    plumbing   = "% Lacking\nPlumbing",
    population = "County\nPopulation",
    region     = "Census\nRegion",
    poverty    = "Economic\nConditions"
  ),
  coords = list(
    x = c(population = 1, region = 2.5, poverty = 2.5, plumbing = 4),
    y = c(population = 1, region = 2,   poverty = 0,   plumbing = 1)
  )
)

ggdag_status(water_dag, use_labels = "label", text = FALSE) +
  theme_dag() +
  guides(color = "none") +
  labs(
    title    = "Causal structure: population size and water insecurity",
    subtitle = "Region and economic conditions are backdoor confounders"
  )

DAG for the relationship between county population size and household water insecurity. Region and poverty (proxied here by total population context) are backdoor confounders — they affect both how large a county is and how much plumbing access it has.

Interpretating the DAG:

The arrow from Region → Population means counties in different parts of the country tend to differ in size. An arrow from Region → % Lacking Plumbing means geography independently shapes infrastructure. Economic Conditions (poverty) also connects both to county size and to plumbing access. So a naive regression of plumbing rates on population size would conflate the infrastructure investment story with these regional and economic patterns. To get the causal estimate, we need to condition on region and economic conditions.

Naive vs. adjusted regression

Now for the modeling. Following the DAG, I run two regressions:

  • Naive: just regress the plumbing rate on log population, ignoring the backdoors.
  • Adjusted: add region fixed effects to close the Region backdoor path. This is the adjustment-based identification strategy from week 7 of the course.
# Naive model — ignores regional confounding
model_naive <- lm_robust(
  percent_lacking_plumbing ~ log_pop,
  data = water_clean)

The naive model says that larger counties have lower plumbing insecurity rates which is technically true as a raw correlation. But once we control for region (closing that backdoor path), the estimate on log population changes. The regional fixed effects absorb the fact that larger counties also tend to be in different parts of the country with different baseline infrastructure. What’s left is closer to a within region comparison: among counties in the same part of the country, do bigger ones have lower rates?

The quartile plot drives home the confounding story from the DAG. In every region, the smallest counties tend to have the highest rates of plumbing insecurity infrastructure investment scales with population. But the level differs dramatically by region: even the largest Southern counties have rates comparable to the smallest Northeastern ones. If you only looked at the raw correlation between population and plumbing access without accounting for region, you’d get a misleading picture of what’s actually driving the variation.

Putting it together: what this tells us about program evaluation

The water insecurity data illustrates something that comes up constantly in program evaluation: the thing you’re interested in measuring doesn’t exist in isolation. A county’s plumbing access rate is the product of its population, its region, its economic history, its political institutions, and probably several other things we can’t measure directly.

Running a naive regression that ignores those backdoor paths produces a number that mixes up the causal effect of population size with all the other things that correlate with it. Conditioning on region drawing from the adjustment-based identification framework closes the most obvious backdoor and gives us a cleaner estimate of the within-region relationship. It’s not a perfect causal identification strategy (we’d need an instrument or a natural experiment to get there), but it’s a meaningful improvement over the naive approach, and the DAG makes it explicit which assumptions we’re making and which backdoors we’ve addressed.

If I were designing an actual program evaluation study using this data, the next step would be to ask: is there any source of exogenous variation in infrastructure spending a federal grant formula, a geographic discontinuity in eligibility, or a policy change with regional variation that could give us a true causal handle on how investment affects plumbing access? That’s the jump from descriptive analysis to credible causal inference, and it’s where the tools from weeks 8–12 of this course would become essential
